HTML & CSS Cheat Sheet

A breif overview of semantic html elements and how to use them.

Why care about semantic html?

A semantic element clearly describes its meaning to both the browser and the developer.
Non-semantic html, like <div> & <span>, is easy to use, but difficult for other developers to understand and pick up quickly.
This can present unseen challenges in the future.
In addition to benefiting other developers, semantic html will also benefit you when you come back to and revamp/refactor your own code.

HTML Tag Description Example
<p> Semantic element representing a paragraph <p>Winston was a great dog. Every day he welcomed his parents, at the door, when they came home. In return, his Mom and Dad made sure to give winston plenty of belly scratches.</p>
<header> Semantic element representing a pages header <header>Winston the Dog</header>
<nav> Semantic element representing a pages navigation section <nav><a>About me</a><a>Life Story</a><a>Favorite Places</a></nav>
<figure> Specifies self-contained content, like illustrations, diagrams, photos, code listings, etc. <figure></figure>
<section> Defines a section in a document. A section is a thematic grouping of content, typically with a heading. <section>Winston the Dog</section>
<article> Specifies independent, self-contained content. An article should make sense on its own. <article>Winston is a 3 y.o goldendoodle that lives in Millersville, PA. He likes walks around the neighborhood and making people feel loved.</article>
<aside> Semantic element representing a pages header <aside>Other doggie parents in our neighborhood often ask his Mommy & Daddy what breed he is. While we were told the he is a goldendoodle, Winston looks more so a setterdoodle.</aside>
<footer> Semantic element representing a pages footer <footer>Contact Me @ winston.the.dog@gmail.com</footer>
